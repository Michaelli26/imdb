Analyzing IMDb Data

I wanted to work with a large dataset and chose to work with movies found in IMDb's dataset. After merging different TSV files with the content I wanted and cleaning up the data, I was left with 522,000 rows and 6 columns. Below are the interesting trends I found.

Number of Movies Produced Every Year

The successful first public screening was of ten short films produced by the Lumière brothers' on December 28, 1895 in Paris. In the early 20th century, the motion picture industry was based in Fort Lee, New Jersey, across the Hudson River from New York. In 1908 the industry was in need of warmer headquarters during the winter and Jacksonville, Florida became ‘The Winter Film Capital of the World’ thanks to its exotic locations, rail access, and cheap labor. Over the next decade 30 silent film companies were established (including MGM) and created a large increase in the amount of movies made. In the 2000s online movie streaming services such as Youtube and Netflix made it even more accessible to watch movies. Along with this rise in movie demand, cheaper camera equipment made it easier for more and more independent filmmakers to produce movies.


Average Movie Rating per year

Since these ratings are from IMDb users, many of the older movies aren’t rated around their release date by the people of that time. With this in mind, the figure above still shows the average rating for a movie produced in any year is around a 6. Over time, the trend shows that users are not as hesitant to give ratings on either end of the 10 point scale.


Average Rating vs Number of Votes

As the number of votes for a movie increases, the rating regresses to the mean with a value between 6-8. Suggesting that movies falling on the extreme ends of the rating system (shown in figure 2) occurred with less popular movies having lower amounts of movie votes.


Movie Running Time vs Year

Unfortunately many early films are categorized as shorts (instead of movies) which made them difficult to compare to modern movies. For this reason, movies before 1915 were dropped from this analysis because short films needed to be removed from the entire dataset. Once short films were removed from the dataset there were only a few films categorized as movies left in the early years that misconstrued the data. For example, in 1899 only one film is categorized as a movie (“The Wrestlers”) which is 135 minutes long and does not accurately represent that average length of a film during that time.


The average running length of a film gradually increased until it remained steady at around 90 minutes.


Number of Votes vs Running Time

Most of the movies produced are between 60-120 minutes long and the ones that get talked about the most are almost all within 70-120 minutes.


Rating vs Running Time

Interestingly, longer movies don’t seem to get low ratings. But keeping the previous figure in mind, they also don’t get talked about as much either.
